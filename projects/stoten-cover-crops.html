Cover Crops Restructure Soil Microbiomes Across Mississippi Agroecosystems

A pooled analysis in which we reanalyzed datasets generated within our lab from multiple cover crop experiments.

Principal Investigator: Dr. Shankar Ganapathi, Assistant Research Professor, IGBB, and PSS, Mississippi State University.

Quick facts
  • Scope: Five independent field trials (2020–2024)
  • Crops: Corn, soybean, sweet potato
  • Analyses: Amplicon sequencing, network ecology, neutral theory, ML biomarkers
Rationale Methodology Contact

Rationale

Cover crops are widely promoted for improving soil structure, nutrient cycling, and water retention; however, their system‑level effects on soil microbial diversity, stability, and function across contrasting environments remain under‑resolved. Mississippi offers a strong gradient in soil texture, fertility history, and management intensity. Rather than treating sites in isolation, I wanted to ask a broader question: Do cover crops consistently “rewire” microbial communities toward resilient, functionally rich states across this landscape?

To address this, I harmonized multiple field datasets into a single analytical framework. The intent was not just to catalog taxonomic shifts, but to connect community assembly processes and network structure to functional potential that matters for producers—carbon turnover, nutrient availability, and suppression of opportunistic pathogens.

Methodology (Brief)

  • Design & scope. Integrated five independent cover‑crop trials (2020–2024) spanning multiple crop rotations and edaphic contexts to increase generalizability.
  • Microbiome profiling. 16S rRNA and ITS2 amplicon sequencing processed via standardized QIIME2/DADA2 pipelines for ASV resolution and taxonomy (SILVA/UNITE).
  • Community ecology. Diversity and β‑diversity analyses paired with Sloan’s neutral model to partition stochastic vs deterministic assembly under cover crops versus controls.
  • Network stability. Co‑occurrence networks assessed for modularity, robustness, and keystone/hub taxa to evaluate putative stability gains under cover‑cropped soils.
  • Functional inference. Tax4Fun2/FAPROTAX/FUNGuild used to link taxa to functional guilds and biogeochemical roles (C/N processes).
  • Biomarker discovery. Machine‑learning classifiers (e.g., Random Forest) trained on harmonized features to identify reproducible bacterial/fungal indicators of cover‑cropped soils.
