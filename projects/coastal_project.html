Project

Transport & Fate of Bacterial Communities in Mississippi Coastal Ecosystems

Understanding how abiotic gradients, nutrient supply, and the estuarine metabolome shape the assembly, structure, and dynamics of free‑living vs aggregate‑associated bacterial communities—and their links to oyster microbiomes.

Quick facts
  • Region: Bay St. Louis, Biloxi Bay, Pascagoula Bay
  • Fractions: Free‑living vs aggregate‑associated bacteria
  • Approaches: Amplicon, metagenomics, environmental metabolomics, SourceTracker

Overview

Problem. Despite the central role of bacteria in coastal ecosystem functioning, nearshore bacterial communities in Mississippi remain largely uncharacterized. Their spatial patterns, temporal dynamics, and ecological drivers are insufficiently known, limiting our ability to anticipate ecosystem responses and manage risks to resources such as oysters.

This page summarizes the project scope, scientific motivations, objectives, and anticipated outcomes.

Basics & Background — Marine Microbiome

  1. Coastal regions are among the most productive ecosystems on the planet due to their position at the land–water interface.
  2. Nearshore waters receive high rates of organic matter and nutrients from rivers and urban centers. These inputs combine with the open ocean to fuel productivity and create variability in physicochemical properties.
  3. High productivity supports vital ecosystem services: bioremediation, nutrient recycling, and culturally/economically valuable fisheries and aquaculture.

Goals & Objectives

Goal: Elucidate ecological mechanisms that shape the diversity and composition of coastal bacteria in Mississippi and regulate ecosystem functions.

Objectives

  1. Characterize nearshore bacterial communities and analyze drivers of their spatial and temporal variability.
  2. Identify major sources of bacterial inputs to the coastal region and the ecological processes that regulate their subsequent fate.
  3. Investigate how the quality and quantity of organic matter vary along the coast and regulate bacterial abundance.

We will leverage variability in abiotic conditions, nutrient availability, and the estuarine metabolome to investigate bacterial community assembly, structure, and dynamics.

  • Spatial dynamics: Examine differences at local scales (within estuaries) and regional scales (across the three bays) to capture fine‑scale heterogeneity and broader biogeographic patterns.
  • Temporal dynamics: Track intra‑annual (seasonal) and inter‑annual trends to assess stability vs variability over time.
  • Assembly drivers: Link community patterns to salinity, temperature, dissolved oxygen, nutrients (N, P, organic C), and the estuarine metabolome.

Aims

Aim 1

Characterize nearshore bacterial communities and analyze the drivers of spatial and temporal variability.

Aim 2

Estimate major sources of bacterial inputs to the coastal region and the ecological processes that regulate their fate in the estuary.

  • Potential sources include the open ocean, terrestrial soils, tidal wetlands, urban centers, wastewater, and riverine inputs.
  • Source identification informs ecological understanding and applied management (e.g., tracing pathways of potential pathogens).
  • Sampling design: 3 replicates per source class (urban soil, wastewater, coastal wetland soil, beach sand, estuarine sediment, riverine sediment, riverine plankton, agricultural soil, barrier island sediments, ocean) per estuary — ~90 potential source communities per estuary.
  • Use SourceTracker to infer the relative influence of source habitats for observed communities.

Aim 3

Investigate the quality and quantity of organic matter (DOM/particulates) along the coast and identify molecules and biogeochemical transformations that structure bacterial communities.

  • DOM quantity/quality governs energy flow through the food web and bacterial growth efficiency.
  • Expect longitudinal gradients in molecular complexity along river→ocean transects that relate to bacterial composition and function.
  • Hypotheses:
    1. Bacterial diversity is positively related to DOM molecular diversity/complexity.
    2. DOM molecular complexity exhibits a longitudinal gradient that aligns with bacterial community shifts.
    3. Community composition and functional genes (metabolic/biogeochemical) align with dominant transformations inferred from high‑resolution metabolomic data.

Methods & Plan

Fractions & Oyster Links

We separately analyze free‑living and aggregate‑associated bacteria. Aggregates bind to particulate organic matter and sink to the benthos where oysters feed. Identifying aggregate‑associated taxa can reveal hotspots of potential pathogens and clarify how riverine transport and environmental conditions regulate their abundance.

  • Free‑living: individual cells suspended in the water column.
  • Aggregate‑associated: cells attached to particulate organic matter; heavier and sink into benthic feeding zones for oysters.

Data Streams

  • Amplicon sequencing for community profiling.
  • Shotgun metagenomics for functional potential and MAGs.
  • Environmental metabolomics (DOM complexity/biogeochemistry).
  • Physicochemical measurements: salinity, temperature, DO, nutrients, etc.
  • Source tracking (SourceTracker) with a comprehensive source library.

Sampling Design & Analysis

  • Spatiotemporal sampling across Bay St. Louis, Biloxi Bay, and Pascagoula Bay.
  • Within‑bay transects from river to ocean; replicate stations to resolve local heterogeneity.
  • Seasonal campaigns plus between‑year comparisons for temporal trends.
  • Statistical links between community structure, metabolome, and abiotic drivers; comparison of free‑living vs aggregate fractions.

Expected Outcomes & Deliverables

  • Baseline inventory of nearshore bacterial diversity and composition along the Mississippi coast.
  • Quantification of spatiotemporal variability and identification of key environmental drivers.
  • Source attribution of bacterial inputs across estuaries (oceanic, riverine, urban, wetland, etc.).
  • Integration of DOM molecular complexity with bacterial composition and functional gene profiles.
  • Risk‑relevant insights for oyster microbiome colonization and potential pathogen hot‑spots (aggregate fraction focus).
  • Open workflows for reproducible community and source‑tracking analyses.

Rationale

  1. Nearshore bacterial communities in Mississippi are largely uncharacterized: spatial/temporal dynamics and drivers remain unknown.
  2. Riverine and urban inputs shape fluxes and composition of organic matter, nutrients, and microorganisms; we link these to bacterial community structure.
  3. Comprehensive studies are lacking for Biloxi and Pascagoula; Bay St. Louis data mostly follow a major environmental disaster. We will generate an updated inventory and enable decade‑scale comparisons to evaluate recovery or divergence among estuaries.
  4. Spatiotemporal sampling reveals whether temporal variation is regionally coherent (e.g., seasonal forcing) or estuary‑specific (local inputs), informing management scales. Partitioning free‑living vs particle‑associated bacteria highlights taxa entering oyster diets and possible disease risks.
