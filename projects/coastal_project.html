Transport & Fate of Bacterial Communities in Mississippi Coastal Ecosystems

A concise description of the scientific context and goals for understanding how hydrological and biogeochemical gradients shape free‑living and aggregate‑associated bacterial communities across Mississippi’s nearshore estuaries.

Overview

Problem. Bacterial communities underpin core coastal ecosystem services—bioremediation, nutrient recycling, and food‑web support—but nearshore Mississippi waters remain poorly characterized. We lack an updated baseline of community diversity and composition across estuaries, a clear view of how abiotic drivers (salinity, temperature, dissolved oxygen) and nutrient regimes shape assembly, and a quantitative understanding of how particulate aggregates may supply bacteria—including potential pathogens—to benthic feeders such as oysters.

By separating free‑living from aggregate‑associated fractions and integrating environmental metabolome information, this project links microbial community trajectories to hydrological inputs (riverine, urban/WWTP, wetland) and estuarine physicochemical variability. The resulting baseline enables comparisons among Bay St. Louis, Biloxi Bay, and Pascagoula Bay and sets the stage for evaluating recovery or divergence relative to historical disturbances.

Basics & Background — Marine Microbiome

  1. Coastal productivity at the land–sea interface. Nearshore waters receive continuous inputs of organic matter and nutrients from rivers and urban centers; mixing with oceanic waters fuels high primary production and strong physicochemical gradients.
  2. Microbial centrality in carbon and nutrient cycling. Heterotrophic bacteria consume dissolved and particulate organic matter, remineralize nutrients, and transform redox‑active compounds—processes that regulate oxygen dynamics and trophic transfer.
  3. Aggregates as microbial hubs. Particulate organic matter (marine snow and estuarine flocs) concentrates cells, enzymes, and substrates, accelerating biogeochemical reactions; aggregates settle to benthic zones where filter feeders (e.g., oysters) ingest them.
  4. Assembly along gradients. Salinity, temperature, dissolved oxygen, and nutrient availability filter communities; riverine pulses and storm events restructure composition at event and seasonal scales.

Goals

Primary goal: Resolve the ecological mechanisms that shape diversity and composition of coastal bacteria in Mississippi and connect them to ecosystem functions and aquaculture‑relevant outcomes (e.g., oyster microbiomes).

  • Characterize nearshore bacterial communities and quantify spatial (within‑ and among‑estuaries) and temporal (seasonal/inter‑annual) variability.
  • Identify and apportion the major sources contributing bacteria to each estuary (oceanic, riverine, wetland, urban/WWTP, agricultural).
  • Relate community structure to abiotic conditions, nutrients (N, P, organic C), and the estuarine metabolome; distinguish free‑living vs aggregate‑associated fractions to assess benthic delivery pathways.
