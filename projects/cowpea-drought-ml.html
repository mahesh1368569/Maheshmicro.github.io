Interpretable ML decodes drought responses in the cowpea rhizoplane

What RF + SHAP reveals about marker taxa under water stress, and how microbial “rescue effects” might be leveraged for crop resilience.

Rationale

Periodic droughts restructure the rhizoplane microbiome and, in turn, plant physiology. Yet most studies either collapse over developmental stages or emphasize diversity metrics without resolving which taxa drive prediction at specific stages and genotypes. This work asks whether interpretable machine learning can expose robust biomarkers of drought response that align with ecological mechanisms (substrate use, enzyme activity, host physiology) and are portable to management.

Why it matters: If we can pinpoint taxa that consistently track or counteract drought effects, we unlock testable inoculant designs and stage‑aware management (e.g., priming during vegetative windows).

Objectives

  1. Quantify how drought and growth stage reshape cowpea rhizoplane community structure and function.
  2. Use an interpretable ML stack (Random Forest + SHAP) to identify marker taxa predictive of drought status and compare with DESeq2‑based differential abundance.
  3. Link key taxa to plant traits (chlorophyll, stomatal conductance, biomass) and soil enzymes (BG, NAG) to generate mechanistic hypotheses.

Lite Methods

  • Design: Two genotypes (ES4, UCR369) × two moisture regimes (control, 50% irrigation) × four stages (V2, V4, R1, R4); 64 rhizosphere/rhizoplane samples.
  • Omics: 16S rRNA (V4) amplicon profiling via QIIME2/DADA2; taxonomy with SILVA 138.2. Community‑level physiological profiling via Biolog EcoPlates (AWCD, substrate groups).
  • Traits & enzymes: Chlorophyll, NFI, gs, transpiration, biomass; BG and NAG assays.
  • Statistics: α/β‑diversity; PERMANOVA; RDA linking taxa ↔ traits/enzymes.
  • ML: Random Forest classification with nested CV; SHAP for feature attribution across taxonomic ranks; cross‑checked against DESeq2.

Key Results

  • Diversity: Drought reduced α‑diversity overall; genotype effects were pronounced, with UCR369 maintaining higher richness than ES4 across stages.
  • Composition: Drought shifted communities toward Gram‑positive phyla (Actinobacteriota ↑, Firmicutes context‑dependent) with Proteobacteria declining in UCR369; ES4 retained Proteobacteria dominance.
  • Function: Biolog profiles indicated increased polymer/amine utilization under drought, consistent with stress‑adapted decomposer strategies.
  • Link to traits: RDA highlighted plant physiology (chlorophyll, NFI, gs, E) and enzymes (BG, NAG) as strong correlates of community shifts.

RF + SHAP Insights

Genus‑level models yielded the best predictive performance for drought status (AUC ≈ 0.87). SHAP values ranked taxa by marginal contribution to predictions, offering local explanations per sample. Convergence between SHAP‑top taxa and DESeq2‑enriched features supports biological signal rather than model artifacts.

  • Top drought‑associated clades: Actinobacteriota (e.g., Streptomyces) and Cyanobacteria, with SHAP highlighting taxa that influence predictions even at moderate relative abundance.
  • Ecological read‑through: Actinobacteria contribute osmolytes, antibiotics, and polymer‑degrading enzymes; Cyanobacteria may support N status and redox buffering, aligning with chlorophyll/NFI correlations.
  • Interpretability win: SHAP explains how much each taxon nudges a sample toward the “drought” vs “control” class—useful for building targeted consortia or stage‑specific interventions.

Microbial Rescue & Stress Tolerance

Microbial rescue posits that resident or introduced microbes buffer host fitness under stress by enhancing nutrient acquisition, adjusting hormone signaling (e.g., IAA, ABA cross‑talk), producing osmoprotectants, and stabilizing carbon use efficiency. Here, enrichment of Actinobacteria and N‑fixing symbionts suggests two rescue routes: (i) metabolic hardening via osmolyte/antibiotic portfolios and polymer degradation; (ii) resource support via improved N economy and rhizosphere priming. RF+SHAP‑nominated taxa provide a shortlist for greenhouse consortia and gnotobiotic validation.

Design hint: Build 2–3 synthetic consortia spanning complementary functions (osmolyte production, polymer degradation, phytohormone modulation, N support). Test stage‑aware inoculation (V2/V4) vs rescue at R1/R4.

Open Questions & Next Hypotheses

  • Stage specificity: Do SHAP‑ranked taxa act consistently across stages, or do we need stage‑targeted consortia for maximal benefit?
  • Absolute vs relative effects: How do absolute abundances (qPCR/flow) change for top taxa, and do they better correlate with physiology than relative abundances?
  • Metabolome links: Which exudate/metabolite families (e.g., phenolics, terpenoids) co‑vary with SHAP‑top taxa under drought? Can we elicit them agronomically?
  • Transferability: Will the RF+SHAP markers generalize across soils/cultivars/sites, or are they largely context‑dependent? Multi‑site harmonized re‑analyses needed.
  • Rescue mechanisms: Can we parse contributions of osmolyte production vs hormone signaling vs polymer degradation using knockout/auxotroph strains?
